1.  Introduction  a.  Problem  Definition  
Water  scarcity  and  inefficient  water  usage  have  become  serious  challenges  in  modern  society,  
especially
 
in
 
shared
 
environments
 
such
 
as
 
hostels,
 
apartments,
 
and
 
educational
 
institutions.
 
In
 
such
 
settings,
 
water
 
is
 
consumed
 
across
 
multiple
 
areas
 
including
 
bathrooms,
 
kitchens,
 
drinking
 
water
 
stations,
 
and
 
utility
 
or
 
plantation
 
zones.
 
However,
 
the
 
absence
 
of
 
proper
 
monitoring
 
systems
 
makes
 
it
 
difficult
 
to
 
track
 
how
 
much
 
water
 
is
 
being
 
used
 
in
 
each
 
area.
 
As
 
a
 
result,
 
water
 
consumption
 
remains
 
largely
 
unregulated
 
and
 
unoptimized.
 
One  of  the  major  issues  is  the  lack  of  real-time  visibility  into  water  usage.  Users  are  
generally
 
unaware
 
of
 
consumption
 
patterns,
 
which
 
leads
 
to
 
excessive
 
usage
 
in
 
certain
 
areas
 
while
 
other
 
areas
 
may
 
experience
 
shortages.
 
Additionally,
 
storage
 
tanks
 
are
 
often
 
managed
 
manually,
 
which
 
can
 
result
 
in
 
frequent
 
problems
 
such
 
as
 
tank
 
overflows
 
or
 
sudden
 
depletion
 
of
 
water.
 
These
 
issues
 
not
 
only
 
lead
 
to
 
wastage
 
of
 
a
 
valuable
 
resource
 
but
 
also
 
create
 
inconvenience
 
for
 
users.
 
Another  significant  limitation  is  the  absence  of  area-wise  consumption  analysis .  Traditional  
water
 
management
 
systems
 
treat
 
water
 
usage
 
as
 
a
 
single
 
aggregated
 
quantity,
 
without
 
distinguishing
 
between
 
different
 
consumption
 
points.
 
This
 
makes
 
it
 
impossible
 
to
 
identify
 
high-usage
 
zones
 
or
 
detect
 
irregular
 
usage
 
patterns.
 
Consequently,
 
there
 
is
 
no
 
data-driven
 
approach
 
to
 
optimize
 
water
 
distribution
 
or
 
encourage
 
responsible
 
consumption
 
behavior.
 
Furthermore,  water  flow  control  in  most  systems  is  manual  and  inefficient.  Users  do  not  have  
the
 
means
 
to
 
regulate
 
water
 
flow
 
precisely
 
based
 
on
 
actual
 
requirements.
 
This
 
often
 
leads
 
to
 
either
 
excessive
 
flow,
 
causing
 
wastage,
 
or
 
insufficient
 
flow,
 
affecting
 
usability.
 
In
 
shared
 
environments,
 
the
 
lack
 
of
 
coordinated
 
control
 
can
 
also
 
result
 
in
 
unfair
 
distribution
 
of
 
water
 
among
 
users.
 
With  the  increasing  demand  for  sustainable  resource  management,  there  is  a  need  for  a  
system
 
that
 
not
 
only
 
monitors
 
water
 
usage
 
but
 
also
 
provides
 
actionable
 
insights
 
and
 
intelligent
 
control
 
mechanisms.
 
An
 
effective
 
solution
 
should
 
be
 
capable
 
of
 
tracking
 
water
 
consumption
 
across
 
multiple
 
areas,
 
analyzing
 
usage
 
patterns
 
in
 
real
 
time,
 
and
 
enabling
 
both
 
manual
 
and
 
automated
 
control
 
of
 
water
 
flow.
 
Therefore,  the  problem  lies  in  the  absence  of  an  integrated,  real-time,  and  intelligent  
water
 
monitoring
 
and
 
management
 
system
 
that
 
can
 
provide
 
area-wise
 
consumption
 
insights,
 
prevent
 
wastage,
 
ensure
 
fair
 
distribution,
 
and
 
support
 
efficient
 
utilization
 
of
 
water
 
resources.
 
Addressing
 
this
 
problem
 
is
 
essential
 
for
 
promoting
 
sustainable
 
practices
 
and
 
improving
 
water
 
management
 
in
 
shared
 
environments.

The  AquaSense  project  focuses  on  developing  an  IoT-based  smart  water  monitoring  and  
management
 
system
 
that

The  project  also  includes  monitoring  of  storage  tank  water  levels,  ensuring  that  water

The

of
 
water

to
 
reduce
 
wastage
 
and
 
ensure
 
fair
 
distribution
 
of
 
water
 
across

The

suggestions
 
based
 
on

and

The  AquaSense  project,  in  its  current  phase,  focuses  on  the  software  development  and

of
 
a

and

The

The  project  began  with  identifying  issues  related  to  water  wastage  and  lack  of  monitoring  in

Functional

for

flow
 
rates,
 
tank
 
level,
 
and
 
system

UI

focused
 
on
 
clarity
 
and
 
real-time

Core  system  logic  was  implemented,  including:

The  system  was  tested  using  simulated  or  real-time  data  to  verify  accuracy,  responsiveness,  
and
 
functionality
 
of
 
features
 
like
 
suggestions
 
and
 
automation.
 
Phase  7:  Documentation  and  Finalization  (Week  15–16)  
Final  documentation,  report  preparation,  abstract  submission,  and  presentation  (PPT)  were  
completed.

e.  Project  Management  Plan  
Comprehensive  Project  Management  Plan  
The  development  of  AquaSense  (Software  Edition)  was  executed  using  a  Modified  Agile-V  
Model,

that
 
each
 
software
 
module
 
was
 
unit-tested
 
against
 
simulated
 
hardware

The

was

Phase  1 :  Architectural  Design  &  Schema  Definition  (Weeks  1-3) :  This  phase  focused  on  
defining
 
the
 
RESTful
 
communication

We
 
established
 
the
 
JSON
 
telemetry

ensuring
 
that
 
variables
 
like
 
flow_rate,
 
tank_level,
 
and
 
valve_state
 
were
 
standardized
 
across
 
the
 
C++
 
simulator
 
and
 
Python
 
backend.
 
Phase  2 :  High-Fidelity  Simulation  Engine  (Weeks  4-7):  Instead  of  physical  sensors,  we  
developed
 
a
 
robust
 
C++
 
Hardware
 
Emulator.
 
This
 
module
 
uses
 
the
 
<chrono>
 
and
 
<random>
 
libraries
 
to
 
mimic
 
real-world
 
sensor
 
jitter
 
and
 
interrupt-driven
 
data
 
pulses.
 
It
 
simulates
 
a
 
multi-area
 
environment
 
(Kitchen,
 
Bath,
 
Garden)
 
by
 
generating
 
independent
 
asynchronous
 
HTTP
 
POST
 
requests.
 
Phase  3:  Logic  Gateway  &  Algorithmic  Refinement  (Weeks  8-11):  Development  of  the  
Flask-based
 
"Central
 
Brain."
 
We
 
implemented
 
the
 
Adaptive
 
Aeration
 
Algorithm,
 
which
 
performs
 
real-time
 
floating-point
 
arithmetic
 
to
 
calculate
 
conservation
 
percentages
 
based
 
on
 
simulated
 
tank
 
depletion
 
rates.
 
Phase  4:  Data  Visualization  &  Frontend  Synthesis  (Weeks  12-14):  Construction  of  the  
Glassmorphic
 
Dashboard.
 
This
 
involved
 
implementing
 
an
 
Asynchronous
 
Polling
 
Engine
 
using
 
JavaScript's
 
Fetch
 
API
 
to
 
prevent
 
UI
 
blocking
 
during
 
high-frequency
 
data
 
ingestion.
 
Phase  5:  Validation  &  Performance  Stress-Testing  (Weeks  15-16):  Final  system-wide  testing.  
We
 
simulated
 
"Worst
 
Case
 
Scenarios,"
 
such
 
as
 
a

burst
 
pipe
 
in
 
two
 
zones
 
and

cut,
 
to
 
verify

system's
 
fail-safe
 
response
 
time
 
and
 
Telegram
 
alert
 
latency.
